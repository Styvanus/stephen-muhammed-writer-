Chapter 1

It was not a free world.

Bloodshed. Slavery. Silence.

The Church ruled with an iron scripture, branding obedience as holiness and truth as heresy. The people lived in shadows, chained by ignorance—a design by the Supreme Church to keep them docile. They were told the Church possessed all knowledge, all divine authority.

And so, they dared not speak. They dared not dream.

But legends have a way of surviving—even when hope dies.

Whispers spoke of a Chosen One, destined to lead humanity into light, to restore free will, to bring forth true worship. Yet decades passed. No saviour came. The people’s faith rotted into apathy. Hope was now a tale mothers told their children to help them sleep through the screams.

But even in the deepest dark...

a spark still burned.

---

Emor – Capital of the World

At the heart of Emor rose the Eminence Grise, a black stone cathedral that pierced the sky. Inside, cloaked figures convened under stained glass windows depicting the Church's divine conquest.

A man in jet-black robes strode into the council chamber.

“Nestor Greene,” a tall figure greeted him.

“Nestor Phillos,” Greene nodded, taking his seat.

The chamber fell into silence as the Doyen entered—an elderly man whose eyes had witnessed too much and forgotten too little. The Nestors stood as one. Only when he sat did they follow.

“Praise The Son,” the Doyen intoned.

“He who ended the chaos and gave us His law,” the council echoed.

“Amen.”

The Doyen’s voice was steady, cold. “The agenda?”

Phillos rose. “There’s unrest in the Islands. Whispers that... the One has come.”

The Doyen raised an eyebrow. “This... One?”

“A rebel. A fool who dares challenge the Church. Like Samael once did.”

Laughter rippled through the chamber.

“Fairy tales,” Greene scoffed. “Myths to pacify the ignorant.”

Phillos’ jaw tightened. “Myth or not, the people stir.”

“Should we dispatch enforcement?” asked the Doyen.

“I say we hunt him,” Phillos urged. “Crush this nonsense before it spreads.”

The Doyen waved a hand. “No. We don’t chase ghosts.”

“But—”

“Enough. You’ll receive men in the morning—to restore order, not chase bedtime stories.”

Dismissed, the council filed out. Phillos lingered, muttering under his breath.

“They laugh now. But soon... they’ll remember this moment.”

—

Brees was a city forgotten by glory but not by control. Nestled on the fringes of the Holy Empire, it lay under the ever-watchful eyes of the Church, quiet and subdued. The streets were narrow and cobbled, flanked by crumbling buildings and flickering streetlamps that cast long shadows at night. There was beauty in its bones, but it had grown weary—like its people. Fear hung in the air like smoke from the chimneys, and sermons echoed louder than laughter. Every square had a watchtower. Every corner whispered secrets. It was a place where rebellion flickered in hearts, but few dared to fan the flame.

Sandra tightened her shawl as she stepped into their humble home. It was too quiet.

“Kyle?” she called out. No answer. “Kyle!”

A creak from the staircase. She turned sharply.

Kyle was tiptoeing toward his room, his boots muddy, his face flushed.

“Where are you going, young man?” she asked, arms crossed.

He froze, then flashed a sheepish grin. “Hi, Mom. Welcome home.”

Public Square, Brees

Kyle paused as a crowd gathered in the town square. Chants and murmurs swelled like a rising storm.

Two guards dragged a girl—his age, defiant-eyed—onto the stage.

A priest stepped up, his robes shimmering under the sun.

“People of Brees!” he boomed. “You know the darkness that once devoured the world. God saved us through His Church. But some wish to return to the old ways.” He gestured at the girl. “This one is a witch!”

The crowd hissed.

“I am not a witch, you sick old pervert!” the girl snapped.

Gasps. Murmurs.

“You all heard her!” the priest bellowed. “She curses even now!”

“AWAY WITH HER HEAD!” the mob roared.

The executioner raised his axe.

Then—a scream. The man dropped his blade, clutching his bleeding eyes.

Chaos erupted. The girl broke free, elbows and knees flying. She vanished into the crowd.

“Seize her!” the priest howled.

---

She ran like fire—dodging alleys, breath ragged—then dove into a trash bin just before the guards thundered past.

Moments later, the lid creaked open.

A boy peered in—Kyle.

Before he could speak, she lunged, pressing a jagged glass shard to his throat.

“Make a sound and I’ll slice you.”

Kyle raised his hands slowly. “I’m not with them.”

“Then why are you following me?”

“I know a place. You can hide there.”

She narrowed her eyes. “Why help me?”

Kyle offered a crooked smile. “Because I hate them too. And right now… you don’t have a better option.”

Sandra’s Home

A knock rattled the front door. Sandra froze, spoon clattering to the floor.

A man stood on her porch in fine crimson robes. His voice was silk-wrapped steel.

“Good evening, Sandra. I’m Jaime—from the Holy City.”

She bowed her head. “Please, come in.”

Inside, he studied the room with detached interest.

“I noticed your son missed school today.”

Sandra’s heart stumbled. “He—he must’ve wandered off. I didn’t know.”

Jaime’s gaze sharpened. “Your family already carries the stain of heresy. The Church will not allow it to spread.”

“I—I take full responsibility.”

“You will.” He drew a candle from his robes, lit it, and held it between them.

Sandra’s breath hitched.

“Begin your penance.”

She slowly raised her palm above the flame. Flesh sizzled. She bit her lip, tears streaking silently.

Jaime finally extinguished the flame. “You may yet be redeemed.”

Later That Night

Kyle found his mother at the table, cradling her bandaged hand.

“What happened?” he whispered.

She hid her face. “Nothing.”

“They came here, didn’t they?”

She turned on him, voice breaking. “They took your father. I won’t let them take you too!”

He wrapped his arms around her. “They won’t. I promise.”

She hugged him with tears flowing down her eyes. "I won't let them."

Sandra was a woman carved by grief and endurance. In her early forties, with warm brown skin weathered by time and sorrow, she carried herself with a quiet dignity. Her eyes—once bright—now held the weight of a thousand silent prayers. Widowhood had aged her prematurely, but there remained a gentleness in the way she moved, a mother’s tenderness that hardship could not steal. She wore plain clothes, always neat, always modest, and a shawl that had once belonged to her husband—a small defiance against forgetting.

Kyle shut his bedroom door and slid the bolt with a soft click.

“You can come out now,” he whispered.

The closet creaked open.

She stepped out—dust clinging to her hair, but her eyes fierce, unafraid.

Kyle tossed her a packet of fried chips. “You hungry?”

She didn’t answer. She just tore it open and devoured them like someone who hadn’t eaten in days.

“What’s your name?” he asked.

“Lily.”

“Where are you from?”

“The Skrit lands.”

Kyle stiffened. “That’s... heretic territory.”

Lily met his gaze, unflinching. “If searching for truth makes me a heretic, then so be it.”

A heavy knock thundered from downstairs.

Then a voice—cold, clipped, and merciless:

“Sandra of Brees. By order of the Holy Guard, we have reason to believe you are harbouring a fugitive. Open this door. Your home will be searched.”

Kyle turned sharply to Lily.

“Not a word,” he breathed.

She froze, eyes wide, breathing shallow.

In that quiet room, between fear and defiance, the fire had found a place to hide.

And maybe—just maybe—

it had found a place to grow.

Chapter 2

The Councilroom was filled with Nestors from different parts of the world. The hall buzzed with noise at first, but when the Doyen arrived, they all stood and remained silent until he took his seat. Once he sat, the Nestors followed suit, and the hall fell into a hush.

A Nestor rose to speak. "For years, there has been controversy concerning the divinity of Mary, the mother of the Son. Some claim she is merely human, others believe she is an Angel, while some assert she is God herself. If we are to maintain our power over the people, we cannot contradict ourselves. We must determine how the Holy Church, the fourth of the Quadrinity and the Bride of Christ, should view Mary." With that, he sat down.

"Thank you, Nestor Anil," the Doyen spoke. "I, too, have pondered this matter for some time, and I believe God calls upon us to decide now."

Another Nestor stood. "Your Eminence and esteemed council, I, Nestor William, support the claim of Mary's divinity. She gave birth to the Son, who is also God; thus, she is the Mother of God. And only divinity can beget divinity."

The hall filled with murmurs. The Doyen cleared his throat, and silence fell once more.

A third Nestor rose. "I am Nestor Chima from the Hotlands, and I oppose the idea of making Mary divine. If we declare her part of the Quadrinity, women will begin to believe they have equal standing with men. They will forget their deeds in the Garden and claim equality with us. If we approve this, I warn you—there will be broken homes."

The Doyen cleared his throat again. "Thank you, Nestors, for your contributions. We cannot deny the divinity of the Mother, but we must ensure that women understand their place in this world—below us!" The Nestors cheered in agreement. "They must know they are unclean and sinful in God's sight. This new belief will not change their standing. We will enforce strict laws on women as we introduce this doctrine to the people."

Then he rose. "Let us pray."

---

Kyle sat in his cell, chained, tired, and hungry. Opposite him sat Lily, and to his right, his mother.

"Mom," Kyle called, but his mother refused to answer. "Mom, I'm sorry."

She finally spoke. "I warned you to stay out of trouble."

"I know, Mom. I just wanted to help."

"By harboring a fugitive in our house?"

"Hello?" Lily interjected.

"No offense," Sandra said to her.

"All taken. I didn’t ask Good Samaritan here to help me."

Sandra glared at Kyle. "Really?"

Suddenly, the guards entered.

"You!" one of them barked, pointing at Sandra. "You have been summoned for trial." They unchained her and took her away, leaving Kyle and Lily alone.

Silence filled the cell until Kyle heard a faint squeaking sound coming from Lily's position. He turned to see her focused on the floor.

"What’s that?" he asked.

"A tunnel that leads outside the city."

"Outside the city, you say?"

Lily smirked and removed a sewer cover, revealing the tunnel entrance. Then, she loosened her chains and tossed the key to Kyle.

"Where did you get this?"

"I have my ways," she replied before slipping into the tunnel.

---

Sandra stood before the Priest of Brees.

"Sandra, daughter of the Church, you have been caught committing a heinous crime against the Church and mankind. What do you have to say for yourself?"

"I plead guilty and pray the Blessed One tempers justice with mercy."

"Mercy? Your crime is against the Church," the priest said. "If you sin against the Father, you shall be forgiven. If you sin against the Son, you shall be forgiven. If you sin against the Holy Spirit, you shall be forgiven. But if you sin against the Church, no mercy shall be granted to you." His voice rang with finality. "Sandra, you will be beheaded by noon."

Sandra’s heart pounded. "Blessed One, please show mercy!" she begged, but the guards dragged her away.

---

The guards returned to the cell but were shocked to find it empty. Alarms blared as some entered the tunnel while others raced to locate its exit.

---

"What about my mother? She's in danger," Kyle said.

"Don't worry," Lily replied. "They won’t kill her. They’ll need her to lure you out."

They trudged through the tunnel toward the exit.

"What brought you to Brees? Your people—Skrit—are enemies here."

"I was always restricted as a girl back home. I wanted to prove I could do more than they believed."

"Are they that restrictive?" Kyle asked, surprised.

"A question from someone whose people don’t even bear surnames because the Church has made them believe they are property? My people are better than yours."

An alarm rang, followed by the sound of footsteps splashing through water.

"They’ve found us! Let’s run!" Lily shouted.

---

They reached the tunnel exit and began climbing up. Lily reached the top first. As Kyle grabbed a handle to pull himself up, it broke loose. He stretched out his arm toward Lily for help.

To his shock, Lily didn’t help. She bent down so only he could hear her.

"The Churchmen aren’t after me. They’ll stop looking if they find you. I’m sorry, Kyle." She handed him a small knife before vanishing.

Kyle barely processed her betrayal before the handle broke completely. He plummeted back into the tunnel. As he staggered to his feet, Churchmen surrounded him.

He raised the knife. "Come near me, and I'll use this."

"Drop the weapon, Kyle," one of them ordered.

He refused. A shot rang out, hitting his shoulder. Pain exploded through him, and he blacked out.

---

Kyle woke up in a bed, his wound bandaged but his body weak. A light-skinned woman entered the room.

"How are you feeling today, Kyle?" she asked.

Kyle grabbed her arm as she prepared an injection. "Please, help me. They’ll kill me."

The nurse hesitated, then stepped back.

Sandra worried for her son. Was he dead? Why had no one told her anything?

Suddenly, the cell gate opened, and Kyle was thrown inside. He groaned in pain.

Sandra rushed to him. "How are you?"

"I’m fine, Mom."

"Where’s Lily? Was she killed?"

"No. She escaped."

"Then why didn’t you?"

At the trial, Sandra and Kyle stood before the court. The hall overflowed with people of all ranks. The Priest addressed the crowd.

"People of Brees, today we witness the judgment of these sinners." He turned to them. "Do you plead guilty?"

Sandra nodded. She turned to Kyle—he was swaying.

"Kyle!" she cried, rushing forward, but he collapsed before she could reach him.

A Churchman examined his body. "He’s dead."

Gasps filled the hall.

"Here is proof! God supports the Church! He punishes those who defy it!" The Priest raised his arms. "Take him away!"

After they carried Kyle off, the Priest faced Sandra. "The Lord has spared you. You are pardoned. But should this happen again, you will be killed on the spot."

Sandra fell to her knees, weeping. "Thank you, Blessed One."

__

Kyle woke up in a moving car.

"Welcome back," said the nurse from the hospital.

"My mother—is she safe?" he asked.

"Yes," replied the driver. "I’m Luke, and she’s Anne."

Kyle exhaled. "Thank you."

---

A voice spoke over Luke’s phone. "Do you have the boy?"

"Yes."

"Good. Bring him to me safely."

"Yes, Nestor Phillos."

Chapter 3

It was midnight. The world lay in slumber, save for the witches.

The coven stirred beneath the moon’s cold glow, their figures shrouded in black robes as they formed a perfect circle. Whispers in a language unknown to mortals echoed through the clearing, weaving an eerie melody into the night. The flames of the bonfire flickered unnaturally, swaying as though they too obeyed the will of the witches.

Elsa, a teenage witch, stepped forward, her heart pounding in her chest. The Head Witch regarded her with knowing eyes before handing her a bowl. She peered inside and smiled.

"This is the blood of our enemies," the Head Witch declared. "Drink it, consume their energy, power, and vitality."

Elsa raised the bowl to her lips, the thick liquid tasting metallic and bitter. She swallowed, feeling a surge of warmth spread through her veins. A strange sensation overtook her—an electric hum coursing through her very being. She wiped her mouth and dropped the bowl.

"Nuel yiu ah enua." The Head Witch’s voice resonated. "Now you are one."

A tremor ran through Elsa’s body. The murmurs of the coven grew louder, urging her to speak. Words formed on her tongue, unfamiliar yet natural.

"Ik fo emer ke fo furk e tig vrok gedom," she said, her voice steady. ("It is an honor to be part of this great coven.")

The witches screeched in celebration, their howls merging with the crackling of the flames.

"Gogelo me gedom, Osgu," the Head Witch welcomed. ("Welcome to the coven, Elsa.")

"Kemigk! Go suss vo em lekusg ko gosofruko ur mog giui!" ("Tonight! We shall feast on mortals to celebrate our new sister!")

Their cries rose to the heavens, a chorus of unholy delight.

---

Luke cursed and kicked the car door. "Damn it! Why now?"

The vehicle had broken down in the middle of nowhere, in a place where even the GPS refused to function.

"What is this place?" Anne asked, peering through the fog-laden trees.

"Beats me," Luke muttered. "But we’re walking from here."

He nudged Kyle awake in the backseat, grabbing his bag. "Let’s move."

Anne hesitated. "Shouldn’t we wait until morning?"

Luke shot her a look. "You can stay if you want."

He started walking, Kyle trailing after him. With a reluctant sigh, Anne hurried to join them.

---

Sandra stepped out of the bathroom and opened her wardrobe. After minutes of indecision, she settled on a blue shirt and black pants. She dressed quickly but paused outside Kyle’s room.

She entered, the scent of his belongings hitting her immediately. Memories flooded her mind—his laughter, his presence. Was he still alive? Or had he already met a tragic fate?

Tears welled up in her eyes. She sat on his bed, running a hand over the sheets.

"Oh, Kyle, be safe," she whispered before wiping her eyes and leaving the room.

---

Elsa flipped through the pages of her spellbook when her mother entered.

"How was your initiation?"

"The usual," Elsa replied.

"And how do you feel?"

Elsa exhaled. "Stronger. There's this power inside me—it feels like I could explode at any moment."

"Give it time. It will settle," her mother reassured. "The Dark Lord never gives more than we can handle."

She placed a kiss on Elsa’s forehead before leaving. Moments later, Frederick entered.

"So? How was it?" he asked.

Elsa smirked. "You’ll see for yourself soon enough."

Fred chuckled. "Tell me, what’s the best part?"

"The blood. Lots of blood."

"Charming," Fred mused. "Learn anything new?"

"Weather manipulation," Elsa said proudly.

"Liar," Fred snorted.

Elsa walked to the window, lifting her hands. "Ruin, ruin, gelo vert um io gum!"

Nothing happened.

Fred burst into laughter. Elsa scowled and hurled her book at him, but he dodged easily.

"Weather manipulation isn’t for amateurs," he teased.

"I’m a witch! I’m not a novice anymore!"

"Take it easy. Pandemonium wasn’t built in a day."

Elsa sighed. "I will."

---

Kyle’s stomach growled. "Luke, I’m starving."

Luke pointed to a bridge ahead. "Hold on until we cross."

Anne frowned. "Luke, he’s just a kid."

"He understands," Luke said firmly. "Right, Kyle?"

Kyle nodded, though hunger gnawed at him.

They reached the bridge. The wood creaked under Luke’s weight as he crossed. Anne hesitated.

"This thing could collapse any second," she muttered.

"It’s the safest route. I’ve crossed it before," Luke assured. "Five, maybe six years ago."

"That’s not reassuring."

"Just come on."

Luke and Anne made it across. Kyle hesitated at the edge. Then, as he took his first step, the wind howled. The sky darkened unnaturally, thunder rolling through the heavens.

Luke looked up. "This isn’t normal."

A storm surged out of nowhere. Heavy winds rocked the bridge.

"Kyle, hurry!" Anne shouted.

Kyle scrambled forward, but the ropes snapped. The bridge collapsed, plunging him into the raging river below.

---

Sandra handed her card to the cashier. The girl swiped it—declined.

"I’m sorry, ma’am. Your account’s empty."

Her heart sank. The Church had drained her funds as penance.

"Let her have the items. I’ll pay," a man behind her offered.

She turned. He was tall, fair-skinned, with piercing black eyes. Handsome, but unfamiliar.

"Thank you," she murmured.

Outside, he approached her again, holding out a pack of chips.

"You forgot this."

She blinked. "I don’t remember buying this."

"You didn’t? Huh… I was sure you did."

She accepted it. "Thank you… Mr.?"

"Nicholas. Nick for short. And you?"

"Sandra."

"Nice name. Do you live nearby?"

"A few blocks away."

"Mind if I visit sometime?"

She smiled. "I could use the company."

They both laughed. "See you around, then."

---

Kyle awoke on the riverbank, soaked and shivering. The storm had vanished, leaving eerie silence in its wake.

His stomach growled again. He stumbled into the forest.

---

Lily dangled upside-down, caught in a trap.

Rustling leaves made her heart race. "Help!"

A moment later, she hit the ground hard. She looked up—and froze.

Kyle.

"Kyle! How’ve you been?"

Kyle scowled and walked past her.

"Still mad? I made a mistake. People make mistakes."

"You betrayed me."

"But you didn’t die, did you?"

Kyle’s stomach rumbled again.

Lily smirked. "There’s a village nearby. Come on."

---

Elsa stepped into the kitchen and froze.

Two strangers—a boy and a girl—were eating her family’s food.

"Who are you?" her voice was calm, but her eyes glowed.

Kyle and Lily gasped, clutching their chests as an invisible force crushed their insides.

They collapsed, coughing blood.

"I’ll ask again. Who are you?"

Chapter 4

"I'll ask again—who are you, and what are you doing here?" Elsa's voice was sharp, her eyes narrowed in suspicion.

Kyle felt his chest tighten, the pain unbearable. Lily, beside him, was no better. They both knew they were at Elsa's mercy.

Then, her familiar—a crow perched on her shoulder—croaked, "Wouldn't it be wiser to hand them over to the coven?"

Elsa considered this and nodded. She retrieved a small box from her pocket, whispering an incantation. "Ovgfum air ferig, sisko ferg."

She dropped the box on the floor. "Get in."

Kyle stared at the tiny box, then back at Elsa, still wincing from the pain. "How is that even possible?"

"I said get in!" she snapped.

With no choice, Kyle hesitantly placed his left foot inside. To his shock, his leg disappeared into it. He turned to Lily, but before he could help her, he lost balance and fell in. Seconds later, Lily followed.

The pain vanished. They were inside the box, looking up at Elsa's towering figure. Then, the lid snapped shut, plunging them into darkness.

---

"We shouldn't have crossed that bridge," Anne muttered, arms folded as she and Luke stood amidst dense foliage. "Now we've lost him."

"It was the shortest way," Luke reasoned. "We'll find him."

"I hope so. I don’t want to face Nestor Phillos’ wrath."

Luke's gaze shifted upward. A thin plume of smoke curled into the sky.

Anne followed his stare. "What is it?"

"Smoke," he said. "There's a village nearby."

"Do you think he's there?"

"Let's hope so."

---

Sandra stood in the kitchen, absentmindedly stirring a pot. No matter how hard she tried, she couldn’t stop thinking about Kyle. Was he still alive? Had she made a mistake letting him go?

The doorbell rang.

She hesitated before answering. "Hey!" Nick greeted with a grin. "I was passing by and thought I’d check on you."

She arched an eyebrow. "I don’t remember telling you where I live."

"I asked around. You’re quite popular."

She smirked and stepped aside. "Come in."

"Thanks."

"Can I get you something?"

"I’m good."

"You can't come into my home and refuse food."

"Really, I’m fine."

"If you say so," she relented, heading back to the kitchen. "Make yourself comfortable."

---

Kyle and Lily sat in silence inside the box, facing each other.

"You might as well say it," Lily finally spoke.

Kyle frowned. "Say what?"

"Whatever’s on your mind. Now’s your best chance."

Kyle exhaled heavily. "The problem is, there’s too much to say. I wouldn’t finish before we’re killed."

"I’m sorry," Lily murmured. "For everything."

He was quiet for a moment before responding. "I could have died, Lily. Did you even think about that?"

"I know I make rash decisions. I regret hurting people. I wish I could make things right."

"Is that the fear of death talking?"

"Do you believe in Heaven and Hell?"

Kyle sighed. "I was raised to."

"As a Skrit, I was taught that science and logic rule over faith. But... what if it’s all true? If Hell exists, I don't want to end up there."

Kyle softened. "No one's dying today, Lily."

Suddenly, the box tilted. They tumbled upward as the lid flew open. Moments later, they crashed onto a cold stone floor.

They looked up—before them was a massive crowd. A grand throne stood in the center, occupied by a regal woman with blood trickling from her lips.

Elsa walked past them and knelt before the queen.

The queen's voice echoed. "Osgu, guk udo ai frigk ver og?" (Elsa, what have you brought us?)

"I vig tig urunig im la elo." (I found these strangers in my home.)

"Gio e toa gelo vrel?" (Where do they come from?)

"I fosado toa fo Skrit." (I believe they are Skrits.)

Kyle stepped forward. "Go for mek Skrits!" (We are not Skrits!)

Gasps rippled through the crowd.

The queen’s eyes narrowed. "A mortal speaks the witches' tongue? Are you one of us?"

"I am not a witch."

"Then how do you understand the desecrated tongue?" Elsa pressed.

Kyle shook his head. "I don’t know. I just do."

The queen pondered. "Give them shelter. Treat them as kin."

"We need to leave," Kyle protested.

"Rest tonight. You will leave in the morning," the queen decreed. "It is an honor to have you."

Kyle wasn’t sure what disturbed him more—the queen’s sudden hospitality or the uneasy feeling in his gut.

---

Sandra studied Nick. "Are you sure you don't want food?"

"I'm sure," he said, tense.

She frowned. "What's wrong?"

Nick stood, peeking out the window. He let the curtain fall and turned to her. "They're watching you."

Sandra’s heart pounded. "Who?"

"The Church. Ron sent me."

Her breath caught. "Ron? He's alive? Safe?"

"Yes, but he’s worried about you. He heard about the trials."

Tears welled in her eyes. "He shouldn’t concern himself with us."

Nick hesitated. "He says he loves you both. Where’s the boy?"

She choked back a sob. "He’s dead."

Nick placed a comforting hand on her shoulder. "I’m sorry."

Suddenly, the door burst open. A Churchman stood there. Before Nick could react, the man slammed his gun handle into Nick’s face. Blood splattered as he collapsed.

The Churchman turned to Sandra. Before he could act, Nick lunged from behind and drove a knife into his back.

Sandra gasped. "You killed a Churchman."

"More will come. We need to leave."

"How? They’re everywhere."

Nick pushed the bookshelf aside, revealing a dark passage.

"How did you—?"

"Ron told me about it. It leads outside Brees."

Sandra hesitated. Brees was her home. But staying meant death.

She exhaled. "Let’s go."

---

Anne and Luke stood on a cliff, overlooking the witches' village.

"Which village is this?" Anne asked.

"The last witch refuge."

"Let’s leave them be."

Luke smirked. "If we wipe them out, the Church will reward us. No more chasing Kyle."

Anne’s eyes gleamed. "Good idea. What’s the plan?"

Luke unsheathed his blade. "We attack at dawn. The witches’ era ends tonight."

Chapter 5

The witches of the village went about their daily routines, oblivious to the doom about to befall them. It was just another ordinary day—until the first raindrop fell.

Tiny droplets kissed the earth before transforming into a relentless downpour. The blonde-haired woman smiled as she lifted her face to the sky, embracing the cool sensation. The villagers welcomed the rain, laughing and dancing beneath the falling water. But their joy was short-lived.

Suddenly, they began coughing violently. Blood splattered onto the wet ground, mingling with the rain. Their bodies convulsed as something wretched clawed its way up their throats. One by one, they vomited lifeless creatures—frogs, crows, vultures—each the very familiars they had bonded with for years. And then, as their familiars perished, so did they. Their bodies crumpled onto the muddied ground, their eyes lifeless.

This was no ordinary rain.

The queen of the witches stepped out of her dwelling, her dark gaze lifting toward the distant hills. There, silhouetted against the stormy sky, stood the Churchmen—rows of armored soldiers wielding weapons blessed with holy power. They descended upon the village like an unrelenting storm, and the queen knew the truth:

Holy water—the one thing witches could not withstand.

---

The Churchmen struck with righteous fury, cutting down every witch in their path. Their blades and bullets met no resistance as the village fell into ruin.
