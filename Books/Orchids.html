Pilot

1000 A.D.

It was a cool Monday morning in the quiet village of Littleton. The sun peeked through soft clouds, casting golden hues on rooftops. Birds chirped in harmony, and the scent of dew still clung to the earth. It was a morning meant for work.

Dan woke slowly, sleep still heavy in his limbs. From the dining room came the soft clatter of plates and the low murmur of voices. He stretched, yawned, and shuffled out of his room.

"Ah, you're awake then, Dan?" his father said, a broad man with a chest like an ox and a beard tumbling down like a waterfall.

"Aye," Dan muttered as he slumped into his chair. "The dream came again."

His mother stepped from the kitchen, her hands still dusted with flour. "The dream again? What was it this time?"

Before Dan could answer, his father did. "The one where he lives a thousand years… yet always ends up alone."

His mother sat beside him and placed her warm hand gently on his. "You're not alone, love. You've got us."

"And no soul lives to see a hundred,” his father added with a wry smile. “So it’s naught but a dream.”

Dan let out a slow breath, the tension slipping from his shoulders.

"Come," his mother said quietly. "Let us pray."

---

After breakfast, life resumed as usual. His father went off to work, his mother to the market. Dan stayed home, restless.

Their house sat near the edge of a forest—an ancient, tangled place his parents had always warned him not to enter.

But curiosity whispered louder than caution.

He stepped into the backyard, past the line he wasn’t meant to cross, and slipped into the trees.

Sunlight filtered through the canopy, casting gold-flecked shadows across the forest floor. Bright flowers lined the path, glowing softly in the morning light. Dan reached out to pluck one.

A sharp snort froze him in place.

He didn’t need to look to know what it was.

The snorting grew louder—faster, heavier.

Dan ran.

A wild warthog burst through the brush, its tusks flashing like blades.

He sprinted, heart pounding, lungs burning—then his foot caught a root.

He fell.

The ground gave way beneath him.

He tumbled into a hidden pit lined with thorns. Pain screamed through his limbs. He couldn’t move. He couldn’t shout.

But he could see.

The warthog stood at the edge of the pit, staring down at him.

And then—unbelievably—it rose.

It stood on two legs, monstrous and near-human.

Dan’s vision spun.

An arrow flew from the trees and struck the creature in the chest.

It screamed—a horrid, piercing sound.

Then a girl stepped from the shadows.

She was draped in white, moving like starlight on water—silent, swift, sure.

With a single stroke, she cut off the creature’s head.

She turned, as if to vanish back into the trees.

Dan groaned. It was all he could manage.

The pain flared again.

She paused, turned back, and looked down at him. She was his age, maybe younger. Her face was calm. Her eyes unreadable.

"Hello," she said, her voice soft as wind in leaves.

And just like that—Dan was no longer in the pit.

He lay on the forest floor.

No thorns. No blood. No pain.

Only her.

“I’ll help you,” she said. “But you must tell no one of this. Not ever.”

She drew a blade, sliced her palm, and let a single drop of blood fall onto his lips.

“This will keep you alive,” she whispered. “Until next we meet.”

Then, without a sound, she vanished.

Darkness took him.

2115 A.D.

Pain. Sirens. Blood.

Dan lay on a stretcher as lights strobed overhead. Then—blackness.

The rhythmic beeping of a heart monitor dragged him back.

His body ached. His vision was fuzzy.

A figure leaned in the doorway—tall, Black, clad in scrubs and a surgeon’s coat.

"Welcome back," the man said.

Dan sat up, yanking off wires. "What the hell happened?"

"You tell me," the doctor replied, folding his arms. "Your body shut down. I had to isolate you. Give you some time."

Dan groaned. "Cheers, I suppose."

“You could start with that,” the doctor smirked.

“What’s for dinner, you knobhead?”

“I cooked. It’s in the fridge.”

Dan walked over to the window, swinging it open. “You know I can’t stand cold food.”

“There’s a microwave.”

“I’ve not the patience for that thing to work its magic.”

The doctor rolled his eyes. “You seriously can’t wait five bloody minutes?”

“Exactly.” Dan grinned. “See you at home, Boyd.”

Boyd nodded once.

Dan stepped onto the sill—and launched skyward in a burst of wind and light.

---

1000 A.D.

“Dan!” The cries echoed through the forest. Lanterns swung in the darkness like fireflies.

His parents led the search, panic carved deep into their faces.

“Dan!” they shouted again.

A faint sound answered.

His father ran ahead—and there he was.

Dan lay still on the forest floor.

His father dropped to his knees, gathering him up. Dan screamed.

“What is it? What’s wrong?” his father cried.

But there was no reply. Only pain.

“Help me! Get him home!”

The villagers rushed in, lifting Dan carefully.

Blood stained his clothes, but no wound could be found.

At home, Dan burned with fever. His breath came shallow. His skin flushed red.

He was slipping.

Falling.

Breaking.

It felt like the end.

But was it?

And We Met..... Again

1000 A.D

Dan twisted on the straw-stuffed bed, sweat clinging to his skin like dew on dying leaves. Every inch of his body screamed—his bones throbbed as though they were being reformed, his skin stung as though scraped from within. His breath hitched in shallow bursts, eyes fluttering open and closed, lost in a haze of pain.

Beside him, Eliza wrung out a cloth in trembling hands, the bowl of violet-tinted water beside her sloshing quietly with every move. She pressed the cloth to her son’s forehead, her lips whispering soft prayers into the air, as if the room itself were listening.

“He is not improving,” she said, her voice almost a whimper.

Desmond stood by the window, the handle of his axe clenched so tightly his knuckles had gone white. The faint golden sunlight spilled across the floor but couldn't chase the shadows from his face. “We do not even know what ails him,” he muttered. “What happened in that cursed wood?”

He turned abruptly, footsteps thudding against the wooden floor as he headed toward the door.

“Desmond, I beg you—please don’t,” Eliza said, rising and catching his arm. Her eyes were red, her voice raw. “He needs you here.”

Desmond’s jaw clenched, his face twisted in anguish. “Our son is dying, Eliza. Dying. And if there is even the faintest hope hidden in that forest, I must find it.”

“And if there is none?” she asked softly, tears trailing down her cheeks. “What if all he has left is us—and you leave him now?”

A weak voice broke through the room like a crack in still glass.

“Mother...?”

They both turned, rushing to his side.

Dan’s face was ghost-pale, lips dry and cracking. His eye sockets had sunken, bruised black like he'd fought through nights of nightmares.

He looked up at them with hollow fear. “What’s happening to me?” he rasped. “Am I to die?”

Eliza bit back a sob and knelt beside him, gripping his clammy hand. “No, sweetheart. You shall not die.”

“Then why... why does it feel as though I am?”

Desmond sank to his knees, his axe forgotten. He placed a rough, calloused hand over his son’s. “You’re changing, son. You’re healing, though it be painful. But when it’s over, I swear, we’ll go see the horse races together. Just like we said.”

Dan’s lips trembled. “You promise?”

“I do,” Desmond whispered, his voice breaking.

A quiet knock came from the door. The doctor entered—a man of few words, his presence calm but heavy. He carried a creaking wooden box, patched and worn like a trusted companion.

“If you’ll pardon me,” he said gently.

Eliza hesitated, then slowly stood with Desmond. They stepped out in silence.

The doctor sat by Dan’s bedside and reached out. “How do you feel today, lad?”

Dan forced a crooked smile. “Strong.”

The doctor touched his forehead and gave a soft sigh. “You’re burning like a forge. Try the truth next time.”

He opened the box, its hinges groaning, revealing a rainbow of small bottles, dried herbs bundled in string, and rows of folded cloths. He selected a deep purple vial and poured it into a bowl of water—the liquid shimmered like moonlight in the mix.

The door opened again.

Dan turned his head weakly.

A girl stepped in.

She looked like she'd been plucked from a dream—her golden hair glittered in the warm light, cascading down her shoulders in waves that shimmered with each movement. She wore a white gown, pure and delicate, that flowed like mist around her feet. There was something unreal about her, something... familiar.

“Anastasia,” the doctor said, visibly relieved. “Thank the stars. I must tend to another. Keep his fever steady.”

She nodded without speaking, moving like wind—graceful and quiet.

The doctor turned to Dan. “This is my daughter. She’ll care for you now.” He gave Dan a soft pat on the head. “She’s a gentler hand than I.”

Then he left, and Dan’s parents rushed to meet him at the door.

“Will he recover?” Eliza asked, desperation in her eyes.

“I’ve done all I can,” the doctor said. “Let the girl do her work. And pray.”

Inside the room, Anastasia dipped the cloth into the bowl, the scent of crushed herbs filling the space. She placed the cloth on Dan’s brow, her hands cool, her touch like calm water.

Dan blinked slowly, searching her face.

“Do I... know you?” he whispered. “You seem... familiar.”

Anastasia’s eyes softened. “Forgive me,” she said, her voice barely a breath. “This was never meant to happen. My blood should have healed you.”

Dan’s breathing quickened. “Has this... ever happened before?”

She paused, squeezing the cloth. “Not in my time. But it is not unheard of.”

Dan’s hand shot up, weak but desperate, and gripped her arm. “Who are you?”

She looked down. “You mean what am I?”

He tightened his grip. “Tell me.”

“I shall,” she said, gently trying to rise. “When you are strong enough.”

“No—now.”

His fingers caught the edge of her gown as she stood, and the fabric tore—a piece of it fluttering into his hand.

It was soft—too soft for earthly cloth—and beautifully woven with fine gold threads that danced in the light. Symbols he didn’t recognize shimmered faintly in the pattern.

Dan stared, heart pounding, wondering what truth he’d just torn into his grasp.

2115 A.D

The piece of cloth lay in his hand.

Still soft.

Still glowing faintly.

Dan sat on his bed, tracing the threads with trembling fingers. Memories poured in—her voice, her touch, her golden hair brushing against his skin.

He closed his eyes.

She was real.

He wrapped the cloth around his wrist like a secret.

Rising, he pulled on a shirt, still silent, still in thought. The morning air was cool, and the quiet buzz of the waking city hummed beyond his window.

Downstairs, Boyd sipped coffee, dressed for work. “Morning,” he said, eyes on the clock.

“Morning,” Dan replied, reaching for his own mug.

“I’m off,” Boyd added, grabbing his keys.

The door clicked shut behind him.

Dan sank into the couch and turned on the TV.

“News from Pishon. The Super Agent—”

He changed the channel.

Silence again.

He sank back, stretching out for the rest he’d promised himself. But peace never came.

His phone rang.

“Hello?”

A frantic voice replied: “We need your help, Dan.”

He didn’t wait. He bolted out the door.

---

The construction site was chaos—sirens in the distance, workers shouting, arms pointing. A man dangled from a high steel beam, feet scrambling for a foothold, hands bleeding where metal bit into skin.

Time slowed.

Dan shot into the air.

A gust of wind tore through the site as he intercepted the falling worker just before impact. He held him tightly, gently guiding their descent.

Gasps filled the air as they landed safely.

“Thank you... thank you,” the man sobbed.

Dan nodded, then stepped aside.

The worker’s wife rushed in, throwing her arms around her husband. Two kids, barely old enough to understand, hugged his legs, tears running down their faces.

Dan watched.

The rescued man stumbled into the arms of his wife, who clutched him as if the world might steal him away again. Tears streamed down her cheeks, soaking into his shirt. Their two children—wide-eyed, fragile things—ran up and wrapped their arms around his legs, burying their faces in him like he was their whole sky.

The man knelt down, arms spreading, and pulled them all close.

Dan stood frozen.

A lump rose in his throat, thick and tight.

He tried to swallow it, but it only grew heavier.

There it was again.

Love.

Family.

Warmth.

Not headlines. Not powers. Not capes or rescues.

Just love—raw and alive and beautiful.

A feeling he hadn’t touched in years.

The kind that made life worth protecting.

And for a moment, he let it wash over him, like sunlight on closed eyes.

But then—

Voices crashed in.

“Dan! Over here!”

“Tell us how you did it!”

“Were you tracking him?”

“Any statements?”

The reporters closed in—shouting, pushing forward, cameras flashing like war drums of attention.

The air tightened.

Dan flinched at every burst of light.

Each question felt like a hand trying to pull him back into something cold.

He didn’t answer. He never did.

He turned, head down, trying to weave through the maze of people, careful with every step.

If he flew, they’d scatter—maybe get hurt.

So he walked.

Breathed.

Focused.

One step at a time.

Then—

He collided.

It wasn’t hard, but enough for both bodies to fall.

Time paused.

He looked down, reaching out instinctively. “I’m sor—”

The words dissolved on his lips.

She looked up.

And the noise—the questions—the flash of the cameras—everything—

vanished.

Her hair shone like it remembered the sun before it ever rose.

Gold with hints of morning.

A single breeze lifted it, strands dancing like silk in slow motion.

Her dress was white—not just in colour, but in presence. Pure, untouched. Like moonlight made cloth.

But it was her eyes that broke him.

Eyes that knew.

Eyes that remembered.

He couldn’t breathe.

It was her.

It was her.

The world fell away.

And in that blink of a moment—

Dan was no longer a man.

He was the boy, trembling in bed, with fever in his blood and fear in his heart.

He was the boy who reached out for a hand in the dark—and found hers.

He was the boy who felt safe—for the first time.

Now here she was.

Real. Solid. Looking at him.

And in her gaze, he was no longer alone.

He was found.

Lost in her eyes.

After we met

---

1000 A.D.

The wind howled, slipping through the cracks of the wooden shutters like a ghost adrift in mourning. Frogs croaked in rhythm with it, their chorus echoing across the rain-damp cobblestone streets of Littleton—empty, still, and watchful.

Inside the cottage, candlelight flickered gently, casting shadows that danced upon the old timber walls. The dining table sat heavy with silence, disturbed only by the soft clinking of cutlery against wooden plates.

Anastasia ate slowly, with purpose. Across from her, the physician—Jon—had yet to touch his meal. His fingers drummed nervously along the edge of the table, eyes clouded with unease.

She paused, set down her spoon, and dabbed her lips with a worn linen cloth.

“What troubles you, Jon?” she asked, her voice smooth but edged with knowing.

“Did you truly have to do it?” His words were low, yet steady.

Her gaze dropped. For a moment, her expression flickered—sorrow, guilt, and resolve.

“He would have died if I hadn’t.”

Jon leaned forward, the candlelight casting deep furrows across his face. “Can you not see what hangs in the balance? The boy is changing.”

“I know,” she said, her tone calm but distant, as if gazing into a future only she could see. “It’s rare. The chances were slim.”

“What will you do now?” His voice wavered. “He puts you at risk.”

She reached for the wooden cup beside her, the cool surface grounding her.

“I’ve faced worse,” she said, then sipped. The candlelight caught the edge of her cheekbone—graceful, composed.

“The villagers will harm you if they find out.”

“I trust the boy,” she replied simply.

“That’s just like you,” he snapped, frustration rising. “Always leaping before you look.”

Anastasia stood. The bench groaned beneath her as she rose, her demeanour shifting from calm to storm.

She slammed her fists against the table—wood split with a resounding CRACK. The candle toppled, snuffing itself out with a hiss.

“Don’t tell me what to do!” she shouted.

Then... silence.

Her breath came heavy. The shattered table lay before her, broken like a felled tree. Her hands trembled as she stepped back, eyes wide—shocked by her own strength.

“I’m sorry,” she whispered, almost to herself.

Jon rose slowly. “Everything I do... is to protect you.”

She couldn’t look at him. Shame coiled inside her chest like smoke.

“I know,” she said softly.

Then, without another word, she turned toward the window. Her eyes glowed faintly. A gust of wind burst inward as she leapt into the night. The curtains flailed, then fell still. All Jon felt was air.

She was gone.

Meanwhile

Dan’s eyes fluttered open.

He gasped, drawing in the cold night air as though it were his first breath. His skin felt strange—alive, humming with something unseen. A quiet force stirred within him, something primal and long forgotten.

The world around him was cloaked in darkness, yet brimming with life. He could hear everything—the faint scrabble of ants beneath the floorboards, the slow, steady heartbeats of his parents down the hall, the whisper of a lizard’s tail brushing along the wall.

It was overwhelming. Beautiful. And terrifying.

Psst.

A whisper came from the window.

Dan turned, startled.

Anastasia stood there, wrapped in moonlight like silver thread. Her eyes held secrets—but also a strange, steady kindness.

“What are you doing here?” he whispered.

“You wonder what’s become of you,” she said calmly.

“I can hear things,” he murmured, wide-eyed. “Too much.”

“You seek answers? Then come.”

She vanished.

Dan hesitated. His breath caught. His mind urged him to stay—but something deeper, ancient and wordless, drew him forward.

“Come now,” her voice returned, coaxing, almost light. “We’ll be back before your parents ever stir.”

He slipped through the window. The grass beneath his feet was cool, wet with dew.

She held out her hand. The moment he took it, warmth surged through him—steady and bright.

They ran.

The wind tugged at her hair, revealing a beauty not of this world. Graceful. Elemental.

They came to a field beneath a starlit sky. Dew shimmered across the earth like scattered stars.

She released his hand.

“I’ve come to guide you,” she said.

“Who are you?” he asked. “Or... what are you?”

“I am an Orchid.”

He frowned. “What does that mean?”

She stepped forward, voice as old as the hills.

“We carry the speed of the cheetah, the flight of the eagle, the sight of the serpent, the strength of the elephant, and the skin of the crocodile.”

He blinked. “We? There are more like you?”

“No,” she replied. “Only me. And now… you.”

He let out a short, sharp laugh. “Surely, you jest.”

“You must tell no one.”

“Even if I believed you, which I don’t…”

“Dan.”

He turned—and stopped.

She was floating. Suspended above the ground, as though the earth itself had let her go.

“As I said,” she smiled. “Flight of the eagle.”

“You can fly?” he whispered.

“So can you.” She landed, light as ash.

“I don’t know…”

“It’s simple,” she said gently. “As Orchids, we draw in dark energy and turn it into light. We cleanse it. For ourselves… and the world.”

“So… you live on dark energy?”

“No. We do not consume it—we transform it. We are balance.”

Dan stepped back. “If you’re right… that means I can fly too?”

“As I do.”

“All I have to do is…”

“Let the energy move through you and—”

Dan took off.

The earth cracked where he had stood, grass scorched, soil torn. He shot into the sky like a star reborn.

“No, no, no—!” Anastasia launched after him, panic rising in her voice.

Dan soared through clouds, suspended between awe and wind. The world opened below him—Littleton, quiet and hidden in the forest.

And above—eternity.

Anastasia reached him.

“You mustn’t leap like that,” she chided, breathless. “We are Orchids. We are not made for destruction. We are peace.”

But Dan’s eyes were filled with wonder.

“It’s beautiful, isn’t it?” she asked.

“From here… it is.”

“Come. There is more I must show you.”

She rose higher into the night.

He followed.

They pierced the sky together, the wind forgotten, the Earth shrinking beneath them. Stars scattered like shards of silver across the vast black canvas. And then—they saw it.

Earth. A fragile marble of blues and greens, glowing softly in the void. Alive. Small. Beautiful.

Dan’s breath caught in his chest.

“That’s… where we live?” he whispered.

“Yes,” Anastasia replied, her voice as gentle as wind through leaves. “But we’re not done yet.”

They rose higher.

The sun loomed ahead—vast and golden, pulsing with living fire. It should have scorched them. But it didn’t. The closer they drew, the warmer Dan felt—not burned, but held.

His eyes widened. “Whoa…”

“He’s my friend,” Anastasia said, smiling faintly.

Dan turned to her, half-laughing. “You can’t be serious.”

“Watch,” she said.

And then—she flew straight into the heart of the sun.

Dan’s heart seized. But she didn’t burn.

Instead, the sun trembled—and spoke. A voice as old as time, deep as the oceans, warm as a memory never quite remembered.

“Anastasia,” said the sun. “You came. It has been long.”

She returned to Dan, eyes glowing like embers. “Come. He wants to meet you.”

Trembling, Dan followed. He braced for pain—but there was none. The warmth wrapped around him like a mother’s arms. Hide of the crocodile, he remembered.

“Hello, Dan,” said the sun kindly.

Dan swallowed. “You… you can speak?”

“I do many things,” the sun chuckled. “I even have a wife. Though we seldom meet.”

Anastasia’s gaze turned soft. “Before creation, the sun and the moon were one. One light. One soul. But fate split them—to balance the world.”

Dan frowned. “That sounds… cruel.”

“No,” the sun answered. “I love her more than words. But we were given a duty—to shine. To guide. One for the day, one for the night. And so we wait. We shine and wait. At the end of time, we will be one again.”

Dan felt something stir in his chest. A strange ache. A quiet wonder. There was something sacred in that promise.

Anastasia turned. “Let’s go home.”

She flew, trailing light behind her like a falling star.

Dan was about to follow when the sun called softly,

“Wait.”

He paused.

“She is a lonely girl, Dan.”

Dan turned slowly. “Anastasia?”

“In a thousand years, none have stood beside her. None have flown with her. She has carried the stars in silence. But now she’s found you.”

The sun’s light dimmed for a moment, filled with sorrow.

“Give her kindness. Give her care. She deserves to be seen… and loved.”

Dan nodded, a lump rising in his throat. “I will. Thank you.”

And then he dove.

---

She hovered in space, watching Earth spin like a lullaby.

Dan joined her, slower now, something quiet blooming inside him.

“What’s next?” he asked softly.

Anastasia turned to him with a mischievous smile. “Next?”

She didn’t answer.

She shoved him.

Dan shouted as he plummeted—through cloud and sky, tumbling, helpless, terrified—until—

CRASH.

The earth split beneath him, a fresh crater forming beside the first.

He groaned as dust rose around him. “Ow…”

She landed beside him, sheepish, lips curled in a guilty smile.

“I didn’t think that through,” she said. “I’m sorry.”

He looked up at her—really looked.

A girl from another age. A being of peace and power. A soul who’d befriended the sun… and chosen him.

He grinned through the pain. “Worth it.”

And for the first time, she smiled like she believed she wasn’t alone.

“I’m really sorry,” she murmured again.

---

2115 A.D.

“I’m so sorry.”

Those words echoed through time.

Dan opened his eyes.

And there she was—her eyes soft and familiar, hair cascading around her face like a silk curtain stirred by unseen wind.

“I can’t see properly without my glasses,” she mumbled, slipping them on.

Then she glanced down.

“Oh no! My project…”

At her feet lay the shattered pieces of a vase. She dropped to her knees, gathering them, her hands trembling slightly.

Dan crouched beside her. “What is it?”

“It was for my school’s arts exhibition,” she said. “I made it in France during the holidays. The materials—clay, pigments—they’re rare. You can only get them there.” She sighed, a little defeated. “It’s not like you can just fly to France and get them for m—”

Her words were cut short by a sudden gust of wind.

Dan had vanished, leaving only a swirl of air in his place.

She blinked behind her glasses. “Right… I stand corrected.”

Scooping up the shards, she turned and continued on her way home.

Her house was tucked downtown, on a street that buzzed with energy—music from open windows, wind chimes on porches, the faint aroma of grilled food wafting through alleyways. As she walked, two kids barreled past, nearly knocking her over. She tightened her grip on the broken pieces and shook her head.

Home was a modest blue house with a garage out front and ivy climbing the walls like time itself had taken root. She pushed the door open, tossed her bag on a chair, and collapsed into it with a groan.

Then—a soft thud on the porch.

She stood, heart fluttering for a reason she couldn’t name, and opened the door.

Dan stood there, holding a vase.

But not just any vase.

It shimmered faintly in the light—glazed with the same blue as the ocean she’d seen in Nice, trimmed with golden flecks and embedded with tiny jewels.

Her mouth fell open.

“Where did you get that?”

“France,” Dan replied casually. “Called in a favour from an old friend.”

She stared at it, stunned. “This is… far too expensive. If I take this to the art fair, I’ll be disqualified—or treated like royalty.”

“Shall I return it, then?”

“No! No, that would be—way too stressful.” She stepped aside. “Please. Come in.”

Dan entered—and instantly recoiled. A sour stench clung to the air like something had died… in socks.

“What is that smell?”

“Oh—no, no—don’t mind that!” She ran toward the couch, frantically scooping up worn clothes. “Laundry day… or week.” She disappeared into the next room, then reemerged with an awkward smile.

Dan shook his head, half amused. “What’s your name again?”

“Anastasia Wellington,” she said, smoothing her hair. “But you can call me Stacey.”

“Stacey,” he repeated. “Nice name.”

“Thanks. And thank you for the vase, seriously.”

“You’re not actually going to use it for the fair, are you?”

She grinned, mischievous. “Winning’s the goal. I’ll do what it takes.”

Dan’s smile faded.

This… wasn’t her. At least, not the her he remembered.

“No. That’s not who you are, Stacey.”

She raised an eyebrow, arms folding. “And how would you know? We met less than an hour ago.”

“Trust me. It’s been… longer than you can imagine.”

Her face darkened slightly. “Have you been following me?”

“As you said,” Dan replied calmly, “we only just met.”

“You creep!” she snapped. “Get out of my house.”

“But—”

“Out!”

Dan stared at her. She was still her, somewhere deep beneath the surface. But this version had been twisted by time—or maybe by forgetting.

He stepped back, the vase still glinting between them like a symbol of a life remembered by one, and lost by the other.

Then he turned… and left.
