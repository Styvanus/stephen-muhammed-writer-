Pilot

It was raining hard. The floor was wet and slippery.

Ann ran with all her strength as a shadowy figure chased after her. She ran until she slipped and fell.

The figure approached with a dagger in hand. He raised it up to strike—but a mysterious laser beam hit his hand. It fell off, and he screamed in pain.

He turned back to see the source of the beam—and there he was, standing tall in his magnificent armour suit, the one and only: The Super Agent of Pishon.

---

Stephanie dropped her pen and sighed as she admired her work. She had just finished Chapter 2 of The Adventures of The SuperAgent, a book she had been working on for over two months.

“Steph! Breakfast’s ready!” her mum called from downstairs.

“Be down in a minute, Mum,” she replied, slipping the pen into the book and closing it before heading down.

The family was already seated at the table when she got there: her mother, stepdad, and her siblings—Jo and Jay.

She took her seat in the second chair next to her stepdad, Mr. Wilson.

“You woke up late,” her mother said. “That’s unlike you.”

“I woke up with the need to write,” she replied.

“This is a Monday morning, Steph,” Mr. Wilson said. “It’s a school day.”

“I’m sorry, Dad.”

---

Meanwhile...

Ann opened her wardrobe and picked out a pink top. She turned to her friend Claire.

“Is this better?” she asked.

“Isn’t it too party-ish?”

“My sweet sixteen’s happening tomorrow. I need to look the part.”

“The part?”

“You know. Hot and sexy.”

“When don’t you look hot and sexy?”

She pulled the shirt over her head. “This is different. I need to be hotter and sexier—if there’s even a word like that—than ever.”

She turned back to the mirror, this time wearing a red top. "This is perfect."

---

Stephanie navigated her way through the busy locker hall to her locker. As she opened it, she could hear boys arguing across from her.

“The SuperAgent’s cooler than The Flare,” the first boy said.

“No, The Flare is cooler,” the second countered.

“You’re just supporting The Flare ’cause he’s American.”

“I’m not being biased. The Flare is cooler.”

Then he saw Simisola Aderigbe—Simi—approaching.

“Let’s ask her. She’s a Pishonite!” he called her over.

“What’s going on?” she asked.

“We’re arguing over who’s better—The SuperAgent or The Flare,” the first boy explained. “We thought your opinion would help.”

“It’s obvious she’ll support The SuperAgent. Not because he’s better, but because she’s from Pishon,” the second boy added.

Simi smiled. “I’m not really a fan of superheroes, but in my opinion, The Flare is better.” Then she walked away.

---

Ann entered the hall, and there was silence as all eyes turned to her. She walked gracefully to Tom, her boyfriend, and kissed him.

“Hey, babe,” he said after the kiss. “You look beautiful today.”

She smiled. “I know.”

“So... are you on for tonight?”

“Let’s make it tomorrow.”

“Why?”

“Tomorrow’s my sweet sixteen. It’ll be more special if we do it then.”

He smiled. “If that will please you, babe.”

---

Stephanie closed her locker with her books in hand. She caught sight of Dayo, dressed in his traditional Pishonite attire. He walked as if the world didn’t matter—or maybe it didn’t.

He walked past her, and she wished she could stop him—just to hear him say something. He had been in Carson High for over two years, but still, she rarely heard him speak or associate with anyone. Stephanie wondered why he was that way.

---

Simi opened her locker and picked a book. Then Alex approached her.

“Hey,” she said.

“How far?” Alex asked. It sounded awkward to her.

“What are you trying to do?” she asked him.

“It’s Pishon pidgin. I learnt it online.”

“I know it’s our pidgin. I’m asking what the endpoint is.”

“So I can talk to your parents when I go for your—”

“Not happening, Alex,” she cut him off.

“We aren’t a thing.”

“We will be soon,” he replied. “Very soon.”

There was a moment of silence before they both burst into laughter.

“For a second there, I took you seriously.”

He laughed nervously. “You should never take me seriously.”

Ann approached them with two girls behind her.

“What are you laughing at?” she asked.

“I made a j—” Alex began, but she cut him off.

“You know what? I don’t care.”

“Then why are you here?”

“Because you were laughing, and I don’t like it when misers laugh,” she replied.

The girls behind her laughed as they walked away with her.

“Ms Her Royal Richness,” Simi mocked under her breath. “I don’t like that girl.”

“What do you mean?” Alex asked jokingly. “Who doesn’t like Ann?”

“I don’t,” Simi answered abruptly. “She’s too full of herself.”

“Is she?”

Simi gave Alex a death stare. “Oh, she is.”

“She’s having her sweet sixteen party tomorrow,” Simi added. “I wish she’d fall down the stairs and break her face.”

“I should talk about that wish, but I’d rather focus on the ‘breaking face’ issue,” Alex said. “I think you actually meant to say neck or leg.”

She stared at him again. “I meant face.”

She started walking to class, and Alex ran after her.

---

Simi stared at her reflection in the restroom mirror. A lot was going through her mind. It was hard on her. She felt like she could break down, but it wasn’t advisable.

Tears rolled down her cheeks as her mind flooded with thoughts.

Then she heard the toilet flush, and the door opened.

Stephanie stepped out.

Simi quickly wiped off her tears—but it was too late.

There was silence for a moment.

“Are you okay?” Stephanie asked.

Simi nodded, not wanting to speak, fearing it would give something away.

“It’s recess and you’re crying in the restroom, but you expect me to believe you’re okay?” Stephanie asked.

“Give it up, Steph,” Ann said as she walked in and went straight to the mirror. She brought out her lip gloss and began using it.

“She said she’s okay, so let it be.”

Simi went for the door. Stephanie tried to stop her but couldn’t.

---

“I want the party to be perfect and without any blemish—just like the Bible says about the sacrificial lamb,” Ann told the party organizers. She had called them to help her prepare for her “Big Sixteen.”

“You Jewish?” the head lady asked.

“No,” she replied with a smile. “I attended Sunday School.”

One of the men moving items tripped and dropped a glass jar. It shattered.

“Oh my God!” Ann exclaimed. “That jar is priceless!”

The man stood up and dusted himself. “I’m sorry, ma’am.”

“Will ‘sorry’ fix that jar?”

The man was silent.

The head organizer, a woman in her early forties with black hair and signs of wrinkles, stepped forward. “We’re sorry. We’ll take it out of his paycheck.”

“That won’t do,” Ann replied. “I don’t want to see him work for you—or you can forget this contract.”

She turned to leave but bumped into another worker.

She faced the head organizer again. “I want him out too.”

“But he didn’t do anything—”

“I know,” Ann smiled. “He’s just too ugly.” Then she left.

The head organizer, Lucy by name, walked to the first worker.

“I’m really sorry. You can take it out of my paych—”

“You’re fired,” Lucy said abruptly. “I’m really sorry, Mr. Wilson.” She patted his back and walked away.

---

“The sky is beautiful tonight,” Simi said to Alex as they walked down the alley.

“Really?” Alex asked, surprised.

She looked at him. “What does that mean?” She slapped his arm jokingly.

“You’re not really the stargazing type.”

“Well, people change, don’t they?”

“Not Alkebulans,” he muttered.

“You know that’s racist, right?” she said.

“Well...”

They took a seat on the sidewalk bench. Alex noticed she was shivering but trying to hide it.

“Here.” He pulled off his jacket, revealing his white inner shirt.

“Thank you,” she said with a smile. Then she held his left arm and rested on it.

“Simi, there’s something I want to te—”

She cut him off. “Can I enjoy this quiet moment?”

“Alright.” He slowly patted her head.

Simi felt comfortable and relaxed with Alex. It was always like that when she was around him. She knew he had feelings for her—but how she wished she could accommodate them.

Tears rolled down her cheeks as she rested on his shoulder.

---

Stephanie dropped her book and climbed into bed. She couldn’t write—she was too distracted by the saga with Simi.

Just as sleep was about to come, there was a knock on her door.

“Who’s there?” Stephanie asked.

“It’s me, Steph.” It was her stepdad.

“Come in,” she said.

He entered and sat at the edge of her bed. He looked drunk.

She was alarmed—her stepdad rarely touched alcohol, let alone drank. She sat beside him.

“You’re drunk, Dad,” she said. “What happened?”

“Am I a good father, Steph?” he asked.

“The best anyone could have—or wish for.”

“I don’t feel I’m enough. Not like your mum, who’s educated and has a good job. So I work multiple jobs to fulfil my duties as a father—to my kids, especially you, my stepdaughter.” He put his palm on her face. “Making sure you get everything you need to be successful is my goal but...” He removed his palm.

“But what, Dad?” she asked gently.

“Today, I lost my job. And I feel I lost my place as head and father of this home,” he said with tears in his eyes. “How can I meet up with the expenses of the home? Your writing materials?”

“No, you didn’t. You might have lost your job, but you’ll get another. And even if you don’t—it doesn’t make you any less of a father.”

He smiled. “I love you, Steph.”

“I love you too, Dad.” She hugged him.

---

Stephanie entered school feeling a bit nervous. She was about to do something she’d never done before. She needed all the courage she could muster.

There was Ann with her entourage, chatting by her locker.

“So the party will be the talk of Carson City for ages,” Ann said as she opened her locker.

She turned and saw Stephanie standing there. Her smile faded.

“Why are you here?”

Stephanie gripped her jacket nervously. “Can we talk?”

“Go on.”

“Privately,” Stephanie requested. “Please?”

Ann refused. “Whatever you want to say, say it here.”

“I came to plead with you to ask the company to reinstate my father.”

Ann smiled. “Why should I?”

Stephanie went on her knees, drawing attention. “I’ll do anything you want.”

“What if I don’t wan—” Then she saw Dayo pass by.

“On second thought…” She helped Stephanie to her feet with a smile. “There’s something you can do for me.” She stared at Dayo.

Chapter 2

Mr. Wilson sat across from the interviewer, his eyes filled with quiet desperation.

“I’m sorry, Mr. Wilson. You’re not exactly the profile we’re looking for,” she said with rehearsed finality.

He blinked. “Please... I have three kids back at home. Is there no way you could help me out?”

She sighed, eyes softening slightly. “I’m sorry. There’s nothing I can do for you.”

Outside, Mr. Wilson stepped into the blinding sun. His shoulders sagged. He opened his wallet—empty.

“Oh, great,” he muttered. “I guess I’ll just have to walk home.” He looked up at the afternoon sky. “Well, at least I’ll get enough vitamin K by the time I get there.”

And so, he began the long walk home.

Tom rubbed his eyes and climbed out of bed, stretching. As he reached for the door, a feminine yawn behind him stopped him in his tracks. He turned around.

A girl was sitting up on his bed—naked.

“Hello?” he said, startled.

They both screamed. She grabbed the blanket and covered herself.

“Alicia? What are you doing here? What happened last night?” he asked.

She glared. “You’re asking me? You’re the one whose bed I woke up in.”

He blinked, trying to process. “You’re naked… what actually happened?”

“We had sex, genius,” she snapped. “Last night. But clearly, It meant nothing to you.” She stormed out, leaving Tom speechless.

24 Hours Earlier

At lunch, Alicia, Ann, and Jenny sat together at their usual table.

“So you sent her after ‘Lonely Kid’?” Alicia asked, raising an eyebrow.

Ann licked her ice cream. “It’s not impossible. It’s not like I asked her to part the Red Sea.”

“I still don’t see the whole aim,” Alicia replied.

Ann leaned over and pinched Alicia’s cheek. “Oh, my little Alicia, you don’t get to know everything. I’m the mastermind here. You and Jenny—my girls—you just have to trust me.”

Tom approached with a tray of food. He cleared his throat.

“Oh, babe, you’re here,” Ann said sweetly.

“Soo... since the table’s full, maybe we should sit somewhere else.”

Ann gave her girls a look. Jenny and Alicia stood and left without a word.

Tom sat down with a sigh. “You didn’t have to do that.”

“It was necessary.”

“They’re human, Ann. You should start treating them like it.”

She rolled her eyes. “Bla bla bla. I treat them more human than I treat others.”

Tom gave up. There was no winning.

---

Simi sat with her food untouched. Alex noticed.

“You gonna eat that?” he asked.

“I don’t really have the appetite,” she said with a small smile.

Alex raised an eyebrow. “It’s not even noon. People actually lose their appetite? How do they survive the rest of the day?”

“It’s not that hard. We do it in my country every Sunday.”

“We fast on Yom Kippur.”

“Who’s Yom Kippur?” she asked playfully.

He chuckled, then remembered. “I need something from you, Sims.”

“I don’t like that nickname. It never ends well.”

“Well… I’m not promising this will.”

“Fire away.”

“My grandma’s coming from Israel. She wants to meet my girlfriend.”

“Well, good luck with that.”

“You’re the girlfriend.”

Simi blinked. “Really? How am I in a relationship without knowing?”

“I told her I had a girlfriend, but she took it literally. Said she’s coming to see you.”

“Why me?”

“I don’t know.” He looked away.

“Alex. What are you not telling me?”

“She wants to see if you’re in line with our customs.”

“Jewish customs?” she asked. “Alex, I’m a Christian. A Jew’s sworn enemy.”

He chuckled. “It sounded like you said juice.”

“Be serious, Alex.”

“I’m sorry. But if you don’t show up, she’ll bring me a bride from Jerusalem.”

“What’s wrong with that?”

“I don’t speak Hebrew.”

She sighed. “Fine. I’ll help you.”

“Thank you.” He stood. “See you later, babe.”

“If you call me that again, I’ll choke you to death.”

---

Stephanie stood outside Dayo’s house, debating whether to knock. Backing out now seemed easier… but she couldn’t. She knocked.

A short, beardless man opened the door.

“You’re here to see Dayo? Are you sure?”

“Yeah… is that a problem?”

He hesitated. “No, come in.” He led her to a couch in a massive living room.

“Tea or coffee?”

“No thanks, I won’t be long.”

“Where I come from, it’s rude to reject drinks when visiting.”

“I’d really appreciate it if you just got Dayo.”

“I’ll be back.”

Upstairs, Dayo lay on his bed in the middle of a nap.

“What is it now, James?” he groaned.

James opened the door. “You have a visitor.”

“Very funny.”

“I’m serious.”

He chuckled. "Seriously?"

James face was stern indicating that he wasn't joking.

Dayo walked to the staircase and froze. A teenage girl was actually sitting in their living room.

“James, why is there a girl in our house?”

No answer.

Stephanie stood and picked up the remote.

“What are you doing here?” Dayo asked.

She turned to see the source of the voice. It was Dayo.

“I came to invite you to Ann’s sweet sixteen.”

“I’m not going.”

“Please, I really need you to be there.”

He frowned. “Need?”

“My dad lost his job. He could get it back if you show up.”

Silence.

“That’s not my problem, Stephanie.”

“Please… I’ll pay you back, I promise.”

“I don’t like crowds or social events.”

“Just this once, Dayo. My dad—”

“I said I won’t do it!” he shouted. “I don’t care about your father, or you, or anyone. You can use the door when you’re ready.”

He walked away leaving her there.

Stephanie sat there feeling hopeless.

---

Later that evening, Simi stood nervously at Alex’s front door in a blue velvet gown.

“You’re here,” Alex said, dressed in a black suit and jeans. “You look beautiful.”

She chuckled. “Aren’t you going to invite me in?”

From inside, an older voice called, “Who’s at the door?”

“It’s her, Grandma.”

His grandmother appeared—late fifties, sharp eyes, Arab accent.

“My dear, what is your name?”

“I was expecting the interview to be inside,” Simi whispered to Alex. Then louder, “Simisola.”

“Alkebulan?” the woman asked. Simi nodded. “What religion?”

“I’m Christian.”

The grandmother turned to Alex. “A Christian?”

Simi tried to soften things. “I’m not that serious, it’s more like a label these days.”

“A Christian without customs? Really?” she scoffed.

“She was the best I could get,” Alex said.

Simi stared at him. “Seriously?”

“Let’s move this indoors,” Alex said quickly.

“If you say so,” his grandmother muttered. “Bring the titled Christian in.”

Simi followed. “This won’t end well.”

---

The Paquin mansion buzzed with energy. Ann’s Sweet Sixteen was in full swing.

Stephanie entered, visibly underdressed and awkward. She searched the crowd until she found Ann surrounded by her clique.

“Why are you here?” Ann asked. “Did you complete your task?”

“I… I couldn’t. He refused.”

Ann laughed. “Are you joking? Then who’s Prince Charming over there?”

Stephanie turned—and saw Dayo, dressed in his usual native wear. Her face lit up.

“You can’t just let her go like that,” James said.

There was something about this kid that attracted Stephanie and she couldn't tell what it was.

Meanwhile, Tom’s car had broken down halfway there. His phone was dead.

“What now?” he muttered. He began walking.

As he passed a house, a fierce bulldog barked. He raised his middle finger at it—just as the gate flung open.

“Lord help me,” he muttered as he turned to run.

---

Inside, Ann was panicking. The couple’s dance had started. Tom hadn’t arrived.

“He’ll be here soon,” Alicia said, but Ann didn’t believe it.

Dayo noticed the mood shift. “Can we dance?” he asked.

Stephanie smiled—and in that moment, he truly saw how beautiful she was.

“I’d love to,” she replied.

---

Dinner at Alex’s house was painfully silent until Grandma spoke.

“So, Simi, how do you worship?”

“Um… in many ways.”

“But Yahweh said only in Jerusalem should He be worshipped.”

“I don’t know about that. Like I said, I’m just… Christian.”

“Do you know we saw Jesus as our brother?”

“That’s why you killed him.”

Grandma turned to Alex. “Get this Gentile out of my house. And don’t bring her back.”

“No,” Alex said firmly. “I’ve let you make a lot of decisions for me. But this one—I’ll make myself.”

“You’d disobey me for this girl?”

“She’s not just any girl. She’s the one I love.”

Simi stood speechless. She ran outside leaving Alex standing there.

She stopped once the house was out of sight. Fell to her knees and cried.

“Simi,” Alex called, breathless. “Why are you crying?”

She stood. “What you said in there… did you mean it?”

“Every word.”

He cupped her face and leaned in. “I love you, Simi.”

Their lips met—but she pulled back.

“I wish it were that easy, Alex. But I can’t. I’d be doing you more harm than good.”

“Simi—”

“Go home, Alex. I’m not the girl of your dreams.”

And with that, she walked away.

Ann sat alone on the edge of the pavement, arms wrapped around herself as the last of the party lights flickered out behind her. The air was cool, her frustration simmering with every passing second. She checked her phone for the fifth time. No calls. No messages. Just silence.

She was about to leave when Tom finally appeared, stumbling into view. His shirt was wrinkled, his hair a mess, and he looked like he’d been through a storm.

“Where have you been?” she asked, her voice sharp with hurt and anger.

Tom ran a hand through his tangled hair. “It’s a long story, babe,” he said, trying to smile. “Has the party ended?”

“It ended an hour ago,” Ann replied coldly. “And my boyfriend was nowhere to be found.”

“I can explain, Ann. It’s not my fault.”

“Oh really?” Her voice rose. “So whose fault is it then? The sun? The moon? The stars?”

“Ann-baby, if you’ll just listen to me—”

“No, Tom.” Her voice cracked, barely above a whisper. “It’s over.”

Tom blinked. “What’s over?”

She looked him dead in the eye. “Whatever we both had.”

Without another word, she turned and walked away, leaving him standing alone in the quiet night.

---

A few blocks away, Dayo and Stephanie walked side by side through a dim alley, the night stretching around them like a velvet curtain. Stephanie was still reeling from the night’s events, but Dayo’s presence grounded her.

“I have something for you,” he said softly, pulling a white envelope from his jacket.

She took it hesitantly. “What’s this?”

“It’s a job offer. An employment letter for your dad—from Stevens Enterprise.”

Stephanie gasped and let out a squeal of joy. “How—how were you able to pull this off?”

“I asked for it from Mr. Pelumi Stevens. The founder and current CEO.”

Her jaw dropped. “Wait. How did you reach him?”

Dayo gave her a look. “The surname. Stevens?”

She froze, connecting the dots. “You’re a Stevens? You’re Pelumi Stevens’ son?”

He nodded, a faint smile playing on his lips.

“If your father is so rich, then… what are you doing here, in Carson City?”

His smile faded. He looked away. “Goodnight, Steph,” he said quietly, walking ahead.

She called after him. “We’ll see tomorrow?”

He paused, turned his head slightly. “We’ll see tomorrow.”

Then he kept walking.

Meanwhile, across town, Tom sat alone in a dim bar, nursing a bottle of alcohol. His eyes were bloodshot, his hair a mess. He stared into nothingness, mumbling to himself.

“How could she?” he whispered. “She dumped me…”

He took another swig straight from the bottle, his voice slurring.

Alice stepped into the bar, scanning the room—and froze when she saw him.

“Tom? What are you doing here so late?”

He barely acknowledged her. “She dumped me…”

Alice walked over, concern on her face. “You’re drunk. We need to get you out of here before something else happens.”

She threw his arm over her shoulder and helped him to his feet.

Tom’s place was a wreck—clothes and books strewn across the floor, the air thick with the scent of unwashed laundry. Alice grimaced as she guided him to the bed, laying him down gently.

She turned to leave.

“Help me up,” he muttered.

She paused, then bent down to help.

Tom pulled her closer and kissed her. For a moment, she froze—then she kissed him back. One thing led to another, and before they knew it, their hormones took over.

That night became a blur of heat, confusion, and reckless passion—two hearts lost, trying to feel whole again.

Chapter 3

Morning in Carson City.

The city hummed to life—cars honked in the distance, the sun filtered through the trees, and life bustled on as usual.

Inside the grand Paquin Mansion, Ann stirred at the sound of a knock.

She groaned, hair a tangled mess. “Come in…” she muttered, barely awake.

The door creaked open, revealing Gretta, the chief maid—and Ann’s lifelong nanny.

“It’s Monday morning, and Miss Ann is still in bed,” Gretta said, hands on hips.

“Let me be, Gret,” Ann mumbled, burying herself deeper under the covers.

Without a word, Gretta strode to the windows and yanked open the curtains. Light burst into the room like a flood.

Ann groaned and shielded her face with a pillow, but it was useless.

“Breakfast will be ready soon,” Gretta said over her shoulder as she left. “Come downstairs.”

Ann sat up sharply, scowling. She dragged herself into the bathroom, fury in every footstep.

---

By the time she reached the dining room, she was fully dressed, lips tight. The long mahogany table stretched endlessly, decked with silverware and untouched food.

She was just about to eat when the doorbell rang.

A flicker of hope lit her face. “Mum? Dad?” She dashed to the door.

But it was just the mailman.

“Ann Paquin?” he asked.

She nodded, her heart already sinking.

He handed her a small envelope, then turned and walked away.

She checked the sender. Her stomach dropped.

Her parents.

She peeled it open. A sleek chip fell into her palm. A hologram message.

She placed the chip into the reader. The room dimmed. The message played.

Her father’s face appeared, smiling through the projection. “Hello, Sweetie. We’re so sorry we couldn’t make it for your birthday. Your mum and I are at a science convention in North Sahara—”

CRASH.

Ann smashed the chip with her cup, glass shattering across the floor.

Gretta rushed out. “What happened?!”

“They don’t care,” Ann said, voice trembling. “My parents don’t care about me.”

“That’s not true, Ann. Papa and Mama love you very much.”

“No, they don’t!” Ann stepped closer to her. “I haven’t seen them in five years, Gretta. Five!”

Gretta tried to speak, but Ann grabbed her car keys and stormed out.

---

Carson High, that same morning.

Tom walked into school, trying to mask the storm inside. He found Jack and Terrence and gave them a quick handshake.

Jack raised an eyebrow. “You alright, man?”

Tom shrugged. “It’s nothing.”

“Tom. We’ve known each other since Year Four. I know when something’s wrong.”

Tom hesitated. Then: “I slept with Alicia last night.”

Jack and Terrence blinked.

“The same night Ann dumped you?” Terrence asked.

Tom nodded.

“Whoa,” Jack muttered.

“It was a mistake,” Tom added. “I didn’t know. Neither did she.”

Just then, his phone buzzed.

He checked it—and froze.

“Oh God.”

“What?” Terrence asked, peering over.

Tom showed them the screen. A video was playing. That video.

“You filmed it?” Terrence asked.

“Of course not! I was drunk!”

Jack squinted. “It’s only three minutes?”

Tom shot him a look. “Really? That’s what you’re worried about?!”

Another notification popped up:

> If you don’t want the video to go public…

Do the right thing.

Terrence exhaled. “You’re in serious trouble, bro.”

Somewhere else in Carson.

Stephanie grabbed her bag, heading for the door—only to find Mr. Wilson already there.

“Hey… Dad?”

“Good morning, Steph. Heading to school?”

“Uh, yeah? Because it’s Monday?”

He grinned. “I’ve got something better.”

“Better than school?”

“I need you to pick up a package for me.”

“Dad, I’ve got class.”

He patted her shoulder. “They’ll wait.”

Ms Savannah’s Office.

Savannah, Ann’s aunt and therapist, had just finished a session. She checked her watch—break time.

The door creaked open. Ann stepped in.

“Ann, it’s my—” Savannah paused when she looked up.

Ann’s eyes were red. Her cheeks blotchy. Silent tears had etched trails on her face.

“They didn’t come, did they?” Savannah asked softly.

Ann nodded.

“Come. Sit down.”
